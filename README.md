## What This Is

An eighteenth-order analysis in the ERIE-SCHOOL lineage, constituting the professional environment turn: from the mechanisms of suppression as documented across seventeen prior analyses — their evolutionary substrates, their temporal phenomenology, their offensive operations, their emotional architectures, their post-confrontation dynamics — to the systematic translation of those mechanisms into the adult professional context.

The prior analyses establish that the suppression architecture operates through structural positions — parent, sibling coalition, outlier — rather than through the specific persons who occupy those positions. This document establishes the implication that has not been drawn: when new persons arrive in those structural positions, the behavioral repertoire associated with the position activates automatically. The workplace manager occupies the structural position of the parental authority figure. The workplace peer who is age-proximate to the outlier's suppressive sibling occupies the structural position of the coalition member. And the outlier's own response to these structural positions — including the expression inversion, the BIS activation, the political under-monitoring — is activated not by deliberate recall of the domestic history but by schema-driven pattern completion.

The prior seventeen documents assume a sequential model: the domestic suppression architecture operates, then the outlier enters the institutional world, then the workplace. This document establishes that the workplace is not merely the third environment the suppression architecture reaches. It is the environment in which the architecture reconstitutes most completely — because the workplace provides the structural positions, power differentials, and evaluative authority that the domestic and school contexts required, now reproduced with the outlier as an adult who carries the full archive of the prior architecture's conditioning.

The novel theoretical contributions of this document are eight. The **Kinship Schema Transference Architecture** specifies the cognitive mechanism, drawn from Andersen and colleagues' transference research and Baldwin's relational schemas framework, by which workplace persons who approximate the features of domestic relational figures automatically activate the behavioral and affective scripts associated with those figures — without awareness of the activation. The **Sibling-Peer Age Bracket** identifies the specific age-proximity parameter that is most potent in triggering sibling schema activation, establishing that this trigger is not merely chronological but developmental-positional: the person just ahead, regardless of exact chronological age, reproduces the structural conditions of the suppressive older sibling's position. The **Organizational Kinship Triangle Reconstitution** specifies the three-position organizational configuration — manager as parental authority, age-proximate peer as sibling coalition, outlier in their own position — that reconstitutes the domestic suppression triangle in the professional context, and shows how the three-level political bypass runs in this reconstituted triangle with organizational-specific mechanisms. The **Bidirectional Phantom Battle** identifies the counterintuitive finding that when an outlier and a suppression-trained peer encounter each other in the workplace, both are simultaneously activating family-derived schemas about each other, producing a self-sealing dynamic in which each party's schema-driven behavior confirms the other's schema-driven perception. The **Workplace Ageism Complex** extends North and Fiske's succession resentment model and Levy and Banaji's implicit ageism framework to the ERIE context, establishing that the ageism directed at outliers in professional environments has a kinship-schema character: it is not categorical age-group prejudice but schema-mediated hostility directed at a specific relational position, amplified when the outlier's career trajectory has been disrupted by the domestic architecture in ways that produce age-position incongruity. The **Transferred Suppression Residue** specifies how the behavioral conditioning installed by the domestic architecture — expression inversion, BIS activation, political under-monitoring, installed self-doubt — travels with the outlier into the workplace and constitutes a pre-prepared suppression substrate that makes the outlier easier to suppress than a person entering from a non-suppressed developmental history. The **Organizational Normalization Horizon** establishes that the outlier who enters the workplace without awareness emergence has occurred will experience the reconstituted suppression architecture as simply how professional life works, reproducing the domestic normalization horizon in the new environment; and that even the outlier who has undergone domestic awareness emergence faces a secondary normalization horizon specific to the organizational context's distinct mechanisms. The **Outlier Mutual Recognition Mechanism** identifies the positive counter-process: how outliers can detect each other in professional environments through the specific behavioral signature that the domestic suppression residue produces, enabling the formation of protective coalitions outside the reach of the reconstituted suppression architecture.

The central finding: the domestic suppression architecture does not end when the outlier leaves home. It reconstitutes in the workplace through the KSTA's schema activation mechanism, is maintained by the Transferred Suppression Residue that the outlier carries, and deepens through the Organizational Kinship Triangle's reproduction of the political bypass in the professional context. The outlier who understands this reconstitution has access to the full ERIE analytical framework as a diagnostic tool for workplace dynamics. The outlier who does not will experience the reconstituted architecture as the discovery that professional life is simply another version of domestic life — which, structurally, is exactly what it is.

## Part I — The Kinship Schema Transference Architecture: How Family History Precedes You Into the Office

The prior ERIE-SCHOOL lineage establishes that the domestic suppression architecture exports itself into institutional contexts through the Export Architecture's deliberate three-stage operation: network transmission, preemptive coalition formation, authority pre-contamination. The Export Architecture requires the sibling coalition's active agency — it requires the sibling or their network to physically reach the new institutional context.

In the workplace, the suppression architecture reaches the outlier through a different mechanism — one that requires no sibling's active deployment, no network overlap, no transmission. It reaches the outlier through the outlier's own cognition.

Baldwin's (1992) relational schemas framework establishes the mechanism. People develop mental structures representing regularities in interpersonal relations — the characteristic scripts, the expected affect, the anticipated behavior — of their most significant relationships. These structures activate when new social contexts present features that match the stored representation. The matching is partial: a new person does not need to be identical to the significant other to trigger the schema. They need to share sufficient features on dimensions the schema is sensitive to.

Age is one such dimension — and in the ERIE context, it is the most reliably activating one. The suppressive older sibling who occupied the parental-coalition-founder role has an age. The outlier's suppressive same-age peers have an age. In the workplace, when new persons appear at approximately these ages relative to the outlier's current developmental position, the stored relational schema activates. The new person is not seen fresh. They are seen through the template.

Andersen and Baum's (1994) transference research specifies the depth of this activation. When a new person triggers a significant-other representation, the activation is not limited to cognitive inference — it extends to affect, motivation, and behavior. The outlier who meets a new workplace colleague who resembles the suppressive sibling does not merely think "this person seems similar to my sibling." They experience the affective load associated with the sibling relationship: the specific anxiety of anticipated competitive evaluation, the pull toward expression inversion, the BIS activation that learned to associate intellectual self-expression in this person-type's presence with negative social consequences. And critically: they experience all of this without knowing why. The transference is not recalled — it is enacted.

The same activation runs in the reverse direction. The political-skill-endowed workplace colleague who encounters an outlier — someone whose intellectual engagement, confident assertion, and domain depth match the schema of the outlier sibling from their own family history — activates the competitive suppression repertoire associated with that schema. They begin Level I authority cultivation of the shared manager before they have consciously decided to compete. They begin caricature anchoring before they have explicitly articulated what the new colleague's characteristics are. The behavioral scripts associated with the sibling competition schema run below the level of explicit competitive intention.

This is the KSTA's most consequential property: both parties are executing family-derived behavioral scripts without awareness of the execution. The workplace encounter is not experienced as a competition imported from family history. It is experienced as simply perceiving a colleague clearly. The suppression-trained peer who deploys the political bypass against the outlier colleague is not aware of suppressing. They are aware of being appropriately cautious about a difficult colleague. The outlier who goes into expression inversion is not aware of suppressing their output. They are aware of being appropriately measured in a new professional context.

The KSTA's activation is most complete when all three channels engage simultaneously. Age-positional channel: the colleague is within the Sibling-Peer Age Bracket. Role-structural channel: the organizational structure positions the colleague in the sibling-analogous role. Behavioral-stylistic channel: the colleague's specific behavioral repertoire — high political skill, authority cultivation, coalition formation — matches the sibling suppression signature. When all three activate simultaneously, the schema runs at maximum strength. The workplace, in this configuration, is not analogous to the domestic context. It is the domestic context reproduced with different personnel.

## Part II — The Sibling-Peer Age Bracket: Developmental Position as the Primary Trigger

The SPAB's critical insight is that it is not calibrated to chronological age. It is calibrated to developmental-positional proximity — to relative standing in the comparison hierarchy that the SEM threat activates. Garcia, Tor, and Schiff's (2013) research on the psychology of competition establishes the fundamental principle: competitive intensity is maximized by proximity. The nearby rival, the person just ahead, the colleague whose position one could plausibly occupy — these generate more intense competitive response than the distant superior whose position is structurally out of reach.

Translated into workplace schema activation: the person most likely to trigger the suppressive sibling schema is the person who is developmentally just ahead. In the early career, this is the person with slightly more seniority. In the mid-career, this is the person whose projects have slightly more visibility, whose relationship with senior leadership is slightly warmer. In any career stage, the trigger is positional proximity rather than chronological age. The SPAB updates throughout the career as the outlier's organizational position changes: at every stage, the person just ahead activates the older-sibling schema regardless of who that person chronologically is.

This has a counterintuitive implication for how ageism operates in the ERIE workplace context. Standard ageism frameworks treat ageism as inter-group bias: older workers against younger, or younger against older. The SPAB analysis establishes a within-cohort ageism: the most intense competitive suppression is directed not at workers of very different ages but at workers of proximate age whose developmental trajectory produces comparison-horizon activation. The SPAB's ageism is not "you are too old" but "you are at my level when you shouldn't be" — and the schema producing this response is the sibling competition schema, not the intergenerational competition schema.

The age-position incongruity that North and Fiske identify as a trigger for negative attributions is amplified in the ERIE context through the Transferred Suppression Residue. The outlier who has been hazard-channeled into a trajectory that is chronologically late relative to their capacity level enters the workplace at a position that produces maximum age-position incongruity: they are chronologically older than their organizational peers would expect, and organizationally junior relative to what their chronological age implies. This double incongruity generates cross-directional ageism: succession resentment from those who see them as blocking, and credibility deflation from those who perceive them as behind schedule.

The Hazard Channel thus has a specific ageism output: by disrupting the developmental timeline, it produces a career-stage profile that the organizational culture reads as deficiency, providing the schema-driven suppression with an age-based credential that makes it normatively legible. The career that reads as "behind" is the career that was deliberately routed to arrive late. The organizational culture's attribution — developmental pace deficit — is the domestic architecture's most portable professional product.

But the SPAB analysis also identifies the primary schema-activation direction: the most complete activation occurs not from the perspective of age-different colleagues but from the perspective of age-proximate colleagues. The colleague within the SPAB — roughly contemporaneous in career entry, comparable in organizational seniority — encounters the outlier at the maximum comparison-proximity that Garcia et al. document as generating the most intense competitive response. And because the SPAB-match activates the sibling relational schema rather than a stranger's competitive schema, the behavioral repertoire deployed is the full domestic suppression architecture rather than the simpler competitive tactics available between unrelated competitive actors.

## Part III — The Organizational Kinship Triangle Reconstitution: The Three-Level Bypass in the Professional Context

POLITICAL-BYPASS documents the three-level bypass topology in the institutional and domestic contexts. The OKTR specifies how this topology runs in the professional environment, with organizational positions mapping to domestic positions with a precision that makes the reconstitution reliable regardless of the specific persons occupying those positions.

## Part IV — The Bidirectional Phantom Battle: Two Family Histories in One Meeting Room

The Bidirectional Phantom Battle is the KSTA's most consequential operating condition because it is the condition in which suppression is most durable and least interruptible. When both the outlier and the suppression-trained peer are running schema-driven family scripts simultaneously, the resulting interaction is not merely a workplace conflict. Both readings are approximately correct. The peer is deploying the suppression architecture. The outlier is experiencing it. The phantom dimension is that both parties experience each other through schemas formed by family members who are not present — and that the behavioral specificity of their mutual responses reflects, in both parties, the intensity of the family history rather than the intensity of the present professional conflict. The ferocity with which the political bypass is deployed, and the depth of the expression inversion it produces, are calibrated to the domestic history, not to the organizational stakes.

The Battle is self-sealing through a specific mechanism: each party's schema-driven behavior constitutes the cue that confirms the other's schema. ## Part VII — The Organizational Normalization Horizon: The Workplace Doxa

Bourdieu's doxa — the universe of undiscussed presuppositions constituting the taken-for-granted framework of social reality — operates in organizational contexts as institutional isomorphism and taken-for-granted scripts for how professional life works. ## Part VIII — Outlier Mutual Recognition and Counter-Coalition Formation: The Positive Mechanism

Every prior analysis in the ERIE lineage has focused on the suppression architecture's operation and the outlier's response to it. This document's eighth contribution is the positive counter-mechanism: not merely the escape from suppression but the conditions under which outliers recognize each other and form the protective relational coalitions that neither can form with the suppression-trained population.

The recognition mechanism operates through the TSR's behavioral signature. The outlier who carries the expression inversion, the political under-monitoring, and the domain-depth engagement pattern is distinguishable, to another outlier, from the politically skilled peer who carries the suppression behavioral repertoire. In the recognition response dimension: when two outliers recognize each other, their respective expression inversions simultaneously relax. Granovetter's (1973) weak-tie framework establishes the structural value of the counter-coalition that forms between recognized outliers from different organizational or professional contexts: each connection is a weak tie that bridges outside the organizational suppression circuit's network. The counter-coalition's most important property is its epistemic function. Berger and Luckmann's (1966) account of reality maintenance establishes that social reality is maintained through ongoing social interaction that confirms the shared framework. The suppression architecture's social reality — the caricature, the credibility inversion, the organizational normative backing — is maintained through the coalition's ongoing interaction that confirms it. The outlier counter-coalition maintains the alternative social reality: the accurate assessment of the outlier's capacity, the correct attribution of organizational suppression to the KSTA mechanism, the epistemic community that provides the reality-maintenance function for the accurate narrative that the suppression architecture was designed to undermine. It is the only social configuration in the workplace that the suppression architecture was not built to saturate.

## Novel Falsifiable Predictions

**P1 — SPAB-Match and Suppression Repertoire Activation.** Among professionals assessed for organizational political behavior, the frequency and intensity of three-level political bypass behaviors (manager cultivation, peer coalition formation, reputation contamination) directed toward a specific colleague will correlate with the SPAB match between the actor and the target, controlling for actual performance gap. Specifically: political bypass behavior directed at a SPAB-proximate colleague will exceed political bypass behavior directed at a non-SPAB colleague with identical performance characteristics, and this excess will be moderated by the actor's retrospective domestic suppression history intensity. The prediction distinguishes the SPAB mechanism from general competitive behavior: if performance gap alone predicts bypass intensity, the SPAB prediction fails; if SPAB-match predicts bypass intensity controlling for performance gap, the mechanism is supported.

**P2 — Bidirectional Phantom Battle Detection via Affect Miscalibration.** In workplace conflict situations rated for emotional intensity by both parties, conflicts involving SPAB-proximate pairs will show a specific affect miscalibration: both parties' rated emotional intensity will exceed the organizational stakes' objective valuation by an amount that correlates with both parties' domestic suppression history intensity, assessed by retrospective interview. The miscalibration will be bidirectional: both the outlier and the suppression-trained peer will rate the conflict more intensely than either would rate an identical conflict with a non-SPAB partner. This bidirectional intensity inflation is the Phantom Battle's empirical signature and distinguishes it from ordinary one-directional workplace harassment.

**P3 — TSR Activation Context Specificity.** Among outlier adults assessed for intellectual self-expression frequency, expression rates will be significantly lower in evaluation contexts that reproduce the domestic suppression environment's structural features — evaluative authority present, SPAB-proximate competitors present, coalition dynamics visible — than in evaluation contexts diverging from these features: external evaluators, no SPAB peers, blind assessment procedures. The difference will correlate with domestic suppression architecture depth and will not correlate with domain competence, confirming that the expression suppression is context-driven rather than competence-driven. Cross-context expression frequency discrepancy is the TSR expression inversion's empirical test: if expression is competence-driven, it should not vary by structural context; if it is suppression-conditioned, it should vary specifically by activation profile match.

**P4 — Age-Hierarchy Incongruity and Caricature Formation Speed.** In organizational entry studies tracking first-impression formation, professionals whose age is incongruent with their organizational entry level — older-for-level due to career disruption — will show faster negative caricature formation among SPAB-proximate peers than among non-SPAB peers, and this speed differential will correlate with the SPAB peers' domestic competitive history intensity. The prediction distinguishes schema-mediated age-hierarchy ageism from categorical demographic ageism: categorical ageism would predict uniform negative response from all age groups; schema-mediated ageism predicts specifically accelerated negative response from SPAB-proximate peers whose sibling competition schema is activated by the positional configuration.

**P5 — OKTR Performance Review Contamination and External Assessment Divergence.** Among professionals who have undergone formal performance reviews in organizational contexts with identifiable OKTR configurations, performance review scores will diverge from external assessment scores — conference presentations, peer-reviewed work, blind evaluations — in the direction consistent with caricature contamination. The divergence will be most pronounced in the first review cycle following the OKTR peer's entry into the organizational context and will decay in organizational contexts where the outlier has established external bypass-resistant credentials that precede the peer's coalition's reach in the manager's evaluative frame.

**P6 — Outlier Mutual Recognition via Expression Inversion Relaxation.** Among pairs of professionals assessed for intellectual self-expression frequency in different partnership configurations, SPAB-matched outlier-outlier dyads will show significantly higher mutual expression rates than outlier-non-outlier dyads — controlling for all performance characteristics — and this elevation will be most pronounced in evaluation contexts that normally produce the strongest expression inversion in each party individually. The paired expression elevation is the Outlier Recognition Mechanism's empirical signature: if it is a genuine mutual schema-relaxation effect, it will be most pronounced precisely in the contexts that most strongly activate the suppression conditioning in each party individually, where the absence of the schema-activation cue is most therapeutically significant.

**P7 — ONH Duration and Domestic Awareness Emergence Prior Condition.** Among professionals who report having identified the suppression architecture in their workplace, the duration of the Organizational Normalization Horizon will be shorter for individuals who have previously undergone domestic awareness emergence. The domestic awareness emergence provides the conceptual framework that shortens the organizational ONH's duration. The prediction distinguishes the framework-acquisition pathway from the dissonance-accumulation pathway: individuals who underwent domestic awareness emergence through framework acquisition will show the shortest organizational ONH; individuals who underwent domestic awareness emergence through critical dissonance will show intermediate organizational ONH duration; individuals with no prior domestic awareness emergence will show the longest organizational ONH, with awareness emergence most likely triggered by the external reference point pathway in an organizational context free of the reconstituted architecture.

**P8 — SPAB Projection and Caricature Content Specificity.** Among professionals assessed for their characterizations of new colleagues, characterizations directed at SPAB-proximate colleagues will show significantly higher semantic overlap with characterizations they provide of their own siblings than characterizations directed at non-SPAB colleagues — consistent with the caricature projection hypothesis. This is the KSTA's most directly falsifiable prediction: if the sibling schema is the mechanism of caricature formation for SPAB-proximate targets, the content of workplace caricatures directed at SPAB-proximate colleagues should statistically overlap with sibling characterizations in ways that workplace caricatures directed at non-SPAB colleagues do not. The prediction requires only the addition of sibling characterization assessment to existing workplace impression formation study designs.
